Prep Data

The data was messy. Participants fill in their info in a text box, which meant spelling errors and naming variations for the same thing (like Fl, FL, FLA, Florida). The hook-up times change if a site admin went in and added a picture to the posting. This usually happened for fish that were weighed, meaning I can no longer see the time when the best fish were caught.

I did my best to clean the data with a brute force method of viewing unique instances and fixing any errors I saw. Once again, 80% of time was dedicated to cleaning the data!

Joining the two datasets was possible using the columns boat_name and annual. I created new columns that help facilitate easier filtering and analysis. I also filtered out unuseable posting activity. To me, relevant activity included billfish species: blue marlin, white marlin, and sailfish. Although “meat fish” like Mahi Mahi, Wahoo, and Tuna can win a lot of money, I chose to ignore them because they are less of a challenge to catch when compared to billfish. Thankfully, there is consistency each year in how they post the activity, which allowed me to easily categorize and flag each posting.

Distribution of boat lengths by annual year

★ insight:

Boat lengths of Participants varies widely from 21 feet to 97 feet! The distriburion has stayed about the same for the past 6 years according to the boxplots. You can also see the bulk of boat lengths reside in the 48-60 foot range, with a mean of 55-56 feet.

Recent tournaments show the distribution getting thicker in the tails and in the first quartile. There are more smaller boats (20-25 foot), bigger boats (80-85 foot), and sub-mid length boats (42-48 foot). You can tell this by looking at the longer whisker lengths and longer boxes.

Extra Info

These boats are trolling 60-100 miles offshore in the Gulf Stream for the fish and the public is tuned in to what’s happening in real time. There are a lot of other rules for the tournament that I won’t get into here.


~ Terms explained:
  • Port: City/State the boat is from
  • Hook Ups: When a fish bites your lure and you are now fighting it. This is refered to as being “Hooked up”.
  • Releases: When the fish is too small to keep, you let it go. This is called a “Release”. This earns you points towards the Release category.
  • Lost: When the fish gets off the hook or the line breaks it’s considered “Lost”.
  • Boated: When the fish is a blue marlin and it is at least 400 pounds or 110 inches long, you can keep it and bring it back to land to be weighed. This is called “Boated”. The heaviest blue marlin wins the tournament (at least, that category of the tournament, which pays out the most money.)
